INTRODUCTION  The electromagnetic characteristics of the soil, or more in general of any media where a ground penetrating radar (GPR) signal propagates, are of great interest within GPR prospecting in many applications fields. In particular, these characteristics, if correctly retrieved, allow not only a correct time depth conversion [1] but also a correct focusing of the buried targets through a migration or, more in general, an inversion algorithm [2]. Moreover, depending on the applications, the characteristics of the medium can be important in themselves and not only in relationships to the reconstruction and interpretation of the buried targets. This can happen e.g. when the â€œfinalâ€ quantity of interest is the moisture content of the soil [3], or the content of some hydrocarbon contaminant [4]. In general, the dielectric permittivity, as well as the electrical conductivity of the embedding medium, depends in a meaningful way, but also in a complicated and often unknown way, on the chemical, physical and mineralogical properties mixture composing the soil at hand. This makes quite hard to get a reliable a-priori knowledge of them. In particular, some experimental values or semi-empirical laws are available [1,4]; nevertheless, they should be considered as reference-average quantities, which are useful in order to test the likelihood of a measure in the field but should not replace it. In particular, the measure of the dielectric permittivity of the soil (thought of as nonmagnetic) can be performed from the same GPR data [5], classically by means of the shape of the diffraction curves or by means of CMP data1. More recently, an â€œauto-focussingâ€ method based on the linear inversion under the Born Approximation of GPR data has  been introduced [5-6]; however, until now, it had never been experimentally validated. Alternatively, the dielectric permittivity can be evaluated through the time domain reflectometry (TDR) technique [8]. In this paper, a comparative experimental evaluation of the dielectric permittivity of dehydrated sand is proposed, where the diffraction curve, the linear inversion method and the TDR technique are compared. Results show an excellent agreement between the diffraction curve method and the linear inversion method; on the other hand, the TDR measurement provides a comparable but lower value of the dielectric permittivity. II.  THE EXPERIMENTAL SETUP  The test has been performed using a plastic tank filled with sand. The sand has been dehydrated at 105o Celsius for 24 hours, in order to make it homogeneous. In particular, drying sand prevents from inhomogeneity due to possible gradients of moisture content. Moreover, the dehydration also reduces the dependence of the permittivity on possible gradients of density. The tank has a double bottom; it is 92 cm long, 45 cm large, and 33 cm deep with respect to its shallower bottom. It has been filled up with the sand, and a metallic bar (with a diameter of 6 mm) has been buried as depicted in fig. 1. More specifically, the bar is buried at the depth of 18 cm from the top of the tank and at the abscissa 44 cm from the left-hand side of the pool. Three repeated GPR B-scans at the air-sand interface have been gathered. The measurements have been performed with an IDS Ris Hi-mode system equipped with an antenna at nominal central frequency of 2 GHz. Each B-scan has a time window of 32 ns, discretised by means of 2048 samples; the length of the B-scan was 92 cm. When moving the antenna on the sand, extreme care was taken in order to pull the antenna at a constant velocity. The repetition of the scan along the same line three times has allowed a test about the uniformity of the antenna movement velocity. Just after the GPR measurement, a TDR measurement has been performed.
